# The Mode That Never Corrects Itself
*A notebook — from Ali Hajimiri's lecture to siliconforge's nine-stage RF Solver, September 2025–July 2026*

Not a lab report. Just the notebook, wrong turns left in. The equations, the bugs, and the final numbers below are all real; the exact dates are close enough.

## Before September

*Added after the fact, because a notebook that starts in the middle of a semester owes the reader at least one paragraph of before. The rest of this document is contemporaneous; this section isn't, and says so.*

The honest starting point is a kid taking apart gadgets that still worked, just to see what was inside them. That habit is the actual reason "Electronics and Communication Engineering" ended up on a college application a few years later, on the theory that it would teach me how real systems get designed instead of just how to break them.

The years before this notebook starts are a string of ideas that all died the same way. A Smart India Hackathon problem on faster, more accurate survivor detection in collapsed-structure search and rescue — drones, infrared imaging, a model trained to pull a real breathing pattern out of thermal noise, enough edge inference that a rescue team gets a location instead of a raw video feed — stalled because drone platforms and edge hardware cost real money that wasn't there. A second SIH problem, catching earthing-system faults before they became fires, stalled at the sensing-and-alerting sketch stage against nothing more dramatic than an ordinary run of semester deadlines. A community-service project in a village with a water-quality problem turned into a genuine interest in wireless sensor networks — how a field of unattended nodes agrees on anything, reliably, without a wire back to civilization — and then stalled for the plainest reason of the three: no one nearby who already knew the area well enough to point me past the first few papers.

Three projects, three different proximate causes, the same underlying shape: each one needed a resource I didn't have — money, time, or someone else's expertise — and each one waited on that resource showing up instead of finding a way around it. The VLSI honors track, running in parallel through all of it, was the one thread that never hit that particular wall, because analog and digital IC design is a subject an open PDK, a simulator, and enough stubbornness can actually teach you without anyone's permission. By the time it had to go into a formal document for an internship application, it came out as one clean, slightly optimistic sentence: preparatory work on a cross-coupled LC VCO for a ~10 Gb/s link, plus EM and parasitic study in ANSYS HFSS.

That sentence did not survive first contact with an actual EM solver. There was no HFSS seat with my name on it either, it turned out — that specific problem doesn't go away just because the subject changed from drones to inductors. What was different this time is where the missing piece actually sat. It wasn't a hardware budget or three years under someone else's supervision; it was one specific, describable, occasionally infuriating piece of software that didn't exist yet, which is a problem you can sit down and start solving the same evening you identify it. That's the real difference this notebook is about — not that this project had no walls, but that every wall it hit turned out to be one I could personally build a door through, on a schedule nobody else was setting. Whether that would hold for nine straight months on top of a full course load, and later an actual job, was for a long time a genuinely open question. (The drones, for what it's worth, never quite let go of me either — that comes back around in May, wearing a different name.)

## September 2025

**Sept 6** — Third year started two weeks ago and I already have a backlog of "watch later" tabs. Tonight, procrastinating a signals assignment, I finally opened one: Ali Hajimiri, Caltech, on oscillator phase noise. Expected a Barkhausen-criterion refresher. Got stopped instead by one sentence: a limit cycle carries two completely different kinds of stability at once. Nudge the amplitude, the circuit fights back and the perturbation dies out in a few cycles. Nudge the *phase*, nothing fights back. Ever. The oscillator just accepts the new phase and keeps going from there, forever.

That's not a metaphor, it's a hard fact you can derive: differentiate the periodicity condition xs(t+T0)=xs(t) and the tangent vector ẋs(t) turns out to be, itself, a periodic solution of the linearized dynamics. One Floquet multiplier sitting at *exactly* 1 — not 0.999-something, exactly. One direction, neutral, forever.

I replayed that stretch of the video about four times. The reason a clock's phase drifts, the reason "phase noise" is even a category of problem separate from "the circuit has noise in it," is one eigenvalue parked on the unit circle.

**Sept 9** — Kept going through the series. Hajimiri's Impulse Sensitivity Function: inject the same charge impulse at two different moments and get two totally different fates. At a zero-crossing (max dV/dt), almost all phase shift. At a voltage peak (waveform momentarily flat), almost all amplitude shift, which the circuit's own nonlinearity mops up in a few cycles anyway. Γ(θ)≈Γmax·sinθ for the simple case. Every noise course I've sat through treats noise as a number you add up. This treats it as a time-varying sensitivity, and it is obviously the more correct way to think about it once you've seen it. I want to compute this for a real circuit, not just read the theorem.

**Sept 22** — Pitched the honors project: proper LC VCO phase-noise characterization, from first principles, not from a black-box PNoise button. Needed a real number, not a symbolic ω0. Landed on 10.25 GHz. Not glamorous — not 60 GHz automotive radar, not comfortable sub-6 GHz Wi-Fi, just an unglamorous corner of X-band — but the IHP SG13G2 130 nm BiCMOS PDK is actually open (real Verilog-A PSP103 compact models, no NDA), and 10.25 GHz sits well inside what that process can do without also fighting fT/fmax headroom on top of everything else. Read enough spec sheets to know that something like −130 dBc/Hz at 1 MHz offset, on a ~10 GHz carrier, is a fairly ordinary industry bar — not heroic, but not free either — which gave me a real target to eventually check my own number against, once a tool existed to produce one.

**Sept 24** — None of Sept 26's sizing math means anything without real numbers underneath it, not textbook ones — actual Q and self-resonant frequency for a spiral I can actually lay out in SG13G2, not a figure borrowed from a different process's app note. The plan, per that internship application from not long before, was ANSYS HFSS for the EM side. The plan and the license pool disagreed, the same way they always seem to. Went with openEMS instead — open-source, LGPL-licensed, a genuine 3D FDTD field solver, no seat to queue for — and spent the day writing the geometry script (turns, trace width, spacing, inner/outer diameter) instead of the phase-noise theory I'd actually sat down to think about. Got back something I could trust: Q in the 10–20 range at 10 GHz for the geometries that actually fit the floorplan, which is the number Sept 26's Rp, and everything built on top of Rp, is quietly resting on. Ran the MOS and varactor characterization against the PSP103 tables that same afternoon too, mostly so gm and Cvar(Vtune) would stop being symbols I was trusting on faith.

**Sept 26** — Before any solver work: ordinary circuit sizing. Barkhausen for the cross-coupled pair, Zin=−2/gm looking into the drains, so gmRp≥2 for startup, with the usual 2–3× margin built in to survive PVT. Amplitude in the current-limited regime, A≈(4/π)·Itail·Rp — the 4/π is just the fundamental Fourier coefficient of the switched square-wave drain current. Sized the tail current off that directly, aiming to park the bias right at the knee between current-limited and voltage-limited, since that's where the figure of merit is best and the cross-coupled devices stay out of triode at the voltage peaks. Tank inductors landed at 250 pH each from the layout exploration, which pins ω0=1/√(LC) down to LC≈2.4×10⁻²² s² — that number is what the switched-cap bank and the varactor actually have to deliver between them. None of this needed a solver. It's the question the course never asked — given this exact orbit, how much phase noise does it actually make, and from which device — that needed one.

## October 2025

**Oct 3** — First real attempt at a phase-noise number. Built the tank plus cross-coupled pair plus tail source in ngspice, ran `.tran`, got a clean sinusoid. Went looking for `.pss`. Doesn't exist. Went looking for `.pnoise`. Doesn't exist either. ngspice has `.noise`, but that's small-signal noise around a fixed DC operating point — the wrong question entirely for a circuit whose only meaningful "operating point" is a whole periodic trajectory with sensitivity that changes every instant along it. Dead end, and an annoying one, because everything else about ngspice — free, scriptable, good enough for early work — was exactly what I wanted.

**Oct 6** — Chased the real tool instead. Cadence Virtuoso with SpectreRF does PSS/Pnoise properly and is exactly the black box I'd have been happy to sanity-check against. Except: seat pool oversubscribed to the tape-out groups, lab hours 10–5 weekdays only, and an honors project is not first in line for licensed-tool priority. Tried a Keysight ADS trial next. Same story, different flavor of "not actually available to you, specifically, for this." Not really angry about it, mostly just annoyed that the physics I'd gotten excited about three weeks earlier was now sitting behind a licensing spreadsheet I had no leverage over.

**Oct 9** — The turn. If nobody's handing me PSS and Pnoise, I'll write them. I already know the object I need — the PPV — and roughly what machinery gets you there — shooting-Newton for the periodic orbit, Floquet theory for the sensitivity — from the lecture series. ngspice ships a shared C library, not just the CLI; I can drive transient simulations from Python over ctypes and build the missing analyses on top, using ngspice for the one thing it's always been good at: telling me the transient response of a netlist. Did not, at the time, appreciate how deep this was about to go.

**Oct 11** — Before writing anything, spent two days finding out what already exists, because reinventing Newton's method from a linear-algebra textbook at 1 a.m. is not actually a good use of anyone's time. Found `ahkab` first — a real, well-built Python SPICE engine with its own shooting-method module — and got excited for about ten minutes until its own documentation said plainly what it does: periodic steady state for *non-autonomous* circuits, driven by an external clock. An oscillator has no external clock; that's the entire problem. Its monodromy matrix never has to survive an eigenvalue sitting on exactly 1, so its Newton step never has to deal with the singularity that's the whole reason Hajimiri's lecture stopped me in the first place. Found `PyHBSim` and its precursor `YalRF` next — a genuinely solid multi-tone harmonic-balance engine, collocation method and all — and read straight through to their own TODO list, which names PSS shooting, autonomous HB, and PPV extraction as the parts that aren't there yet. Found MAPP last, out of Berkeley — the real thing, full PPV/Floquet machinery, written by people who actually wrote the papers this project runs on — except it's MATLAB, coupled to its own proprietary device-model format, not Python, not talking to Xyce or an open PDK.

So "I'll write them" from two days ago was optimistic phrasing. What it actually means: take the closest existing piece for each stage — ahkab's shooting scaffold, YalRF's collocation approach for HB, the general shape of a reference GMRES/Arnoldi implementation for the Krylov solver — and spend however long it takes making each one do the one thing none of them do on their own, which is survive an oscillator that has no external time reference to lean on. Less "wrote a solver from nothing," more "found the closest thing that already existed for each piece and fought it until it stopped assuming a clock I don't have."

**Oct 14** — Looked hard at PySpice first — a genuinely good, well-maintained Xyce/ngspice wrapper — and confirmed it solves an adjacent but different problem: launching an analysis and parsing the waveforms back, not holding a live state vector still while an outer Newton loop injects a candidate x0 and reads back exactly φ(T,x0) without resetting the DC operating point in between. Needed a narrower, stricter contract than that. Before writing a single solver line, wrote an interface: an abstract `Simulator` class — `load`, `reset`, `operating_point`, `transient`, `inject_state`, `get_vector`, `last_benchmark` — and a rule that every algorithm above it talks only to that, never to the ctypes calls directly. Got burned once already on the MEGHADUT telemetry pipeline by wiring algorithm logic straight into an I/O layer and hating it later. Also thinking, even this early, about what I actually want the finished thing to look like: not one clever script that does everything and shows nothing, but a set of small, separately runnable stages that each do one job and leave a record. That's the difference I actually cared about, more than PSS or Pnoise specifically — most phase-noise work outside a commercial tool is scattered, a notebook here, a one-off script there. I wanted one pipeline, numbered.

**Oct 19** — First backend, and it isn't the ngspice one. A `ReferenceOdeBackend`: the ideal, lossless two-state LC tank, x=[vC, iL], integrated in pure Python, with a known closed-form limit cycle and a known closed-form PPV, v1(t) = [sinω0t /(CAω0), −cosω0t/(Aω0)]. The most boring circuit I could pick, on purpose. If shooting-Newton and the PPV extractor can't nail this to machine precision, there's no point pointing either at a real transistor netlist yet.

## November 2025

**Nov 4** — Shooting method, attempt one: solve ϕ(T,x0)−x0=0 by Newton's method. Doesn't converge. Printed the Jacobian (Φ−I) at the current iterate — one eigenvalue sitting right on 1.0. The Newton step matrix is singular. Spent a genuinely confused hour hunting for a sign error before it clicked: this is the neutral phase mode from the Hajimiri lecture, showing up not as a philosophical point about oscillators in general but as a literal singular matrix in front of me, tonight. Good feeling, that one — an abstract fact from a video eight weeks earlier reaching out to explain a concrete bug.

Fixed with a Poincaré section: pin one state to a reference-node zero-crossing, drop it from the unknown vector, solve Newton over the remaining N−1 states. Reduced Jacobian nonsingular. Quadratic convergence.

**Nov 8** — Second, dumber trap, same week: seeded Newton from the literal DC state to save a step. "Converged" instantly, because x(T)=x0=0 trivially satisfies the shooting condition at the unstable DC equilibrium — a real solution of ẋ=f(x), just not the one I want. Needed a small symmetry-breaking sinusoidal seed (~0.1 units) to knock the iterate off the fake fixed point and onto the actual limit cycle. Left myself a comment above the seed line: *if the guess starts at equilibrium, the residual lies to you.*

## December 2025

**Dec 2** — Built the direct-injection PPV/ISF extractor: calibrated charge pulse (settled on ~5 fC — big enough that the resulting zero-crossing shift clears the integrator's own tolerance, small enough to stay linear), sweep the injection phase across one period, locate the shifted zero-crossing with cubic-spline interpolation for sub-picosecond timing, divide by the injected charge. A flat, uniform phase sweep missed the interesting structure entirely — the ISF is steepest right at the zero-crossings — so switched to densifying the sample phases adaptively by |dV/dt|.

**Dec 5** — First real look: near-sinusoidal, peaking hard at the zero-crossings, nearly flat at the extremes. Exactly the picture from September. Ran the DC-coefficient audit the pipeline was already set up to do, |Γdc|/Γrms, and landed well under the 0.01 threshold I'd set for "this waveform isn't going to have a flicker up-conversion problem." A relief — a badly asymmetric ISF would have meant redesigning the differential pair's rise/fall balance, not tuning a solver parameter. Different feeling entirely, watching your own code independently reproduce a shape you first saw in someone else's lecture slide. Banked it, went back to finals for a week.

## January 2026

**Jan 8** — Direct injection is a measurement, not a proof. Wanted a second, independent derivation of the same PPV, so I turned to the Floquet adjoint equation, v̇1(t) = −Jᵀ(t)·v1(t), normalized by biorthogonality, v1ᵀ(t)·ẋs(t) = 1 for all t. Coded it up, integrated forward in time out of habit — that's how everything else in this project integrates. Numbers diverged almost immediately. Not "a little off." Blowing up.

**Jan 9** — Lost most of a day assuming a Jacobian bug before sitting back down with the theory: the transpose-and-negate flips the whole stable/unstable structure. Modes that decay going forward in the real variational equation grow going forward in the adjoint one. The periodic solution I want is the unstable direction if I integrate it forward — of course it diverges. Rewrote it as a boundary-value problem: integrate backward from T0 to 0 (equivalently, take the backward monodromy matrix's eigenvector at μ=1). Clean, periodic, stable. Filed this one under "bugs that look like noise, not like bugs" — the output isn't obviously wrong the way a NaN is; it just quietly diverges in a way you could mistake for a badly-conditioned-but-working integration, if you didn't already know a genuinely periodic answer had to exist.

**Jan 12** — Validation night. Backward-integrated adjoint PPV against the closed-form ideal-LC analytic PPV, same two-state toy from October: L2 and L∞ error, machine precision. Also ran it against Van der Pol as a second, genuinely nonlinear benchmark, since the ideal LC tank is linear enough that one clean pass didn't fully convince me. Both passed. Rigorous method and empirical method now agree with each other and with the one case I know the exact answer for. First night I actually believed my own PPV extractor.

## February 2026

**Feb 3** — Swapped the toy ODE for a real multi-node circuit through ngspice's shared library. Forming Φ explicitly by finite differences costs one perturbed transient per state — fine for two states, ruinous once the real cross-coupled pair plus parasitics pushes past a dozen. Went matrix-free: never form Φ, only ever compute (Φ−I)v via one directional finite difference, hand it to GMRES. Pulled a reference Arnoldi/GMRES implementation apart and rebuilt the orthogonalization and restarted least-squares solve as one explicit Python loop instead of calling scipy's version as a black box — wanted to see the Hessenberg matrix being built, one column at a time, and needed to wire in my own matrix-free directional derivative in place of whatever matrix format a general-purpose call expects.

**Feb 5** — Tried the complex-step trick, Im[f(x+iεej)]/ε, for a machine-precision Jacobian-vector product with no subtractive cancellation. Excited for about a day, then found ngspice's shared-library evaluation path doesn't take complex perturbations cleanly through some of the underlying model code. Built a real-finite-difference fallback and lived with the consequence: real FD tops out around half machine precision, ~1e-8, which for a high-Q oscillator is enough slop to nudge the phase-mode Floquet multiplier off exactly 1.000 and occasionally ill-condition the Krylov subspace. Added damping and a pseudo-inverse fallback on the reduced Newton system instead of pretending this wasn't real. It's the part of the pipeline I trust least, and I said so in the write-up later.

**Feb 14** — The bug that ate a weekend. Reading the tank's differential capacitor voltage — v(tank,0) — straight out of ngspice's shared-library vector table returned garbage. Two days convinced it was a sign or scaling error in my own Newton loop, staring at residual plots that made no sense. Turned out the shared-library vector database only exposes single-ended, named vectors — v(out_p), v(out_n) — never a differential expression built on the fly. Fix, once found: query both single-ended nodes and subtract in Python. Around the same time also tripped over SPICE being case-insensitive while the internal vector database isn't (C1 in the netlist becomes c1 in the results) — folded both fixes into one small state-reading abstraction so no algorithm above it has to think about either gotcha again.

## March 2026

**Mar 2** — ngspice's built-in models are fine for algorithm development but they aren't PSP103, and PSP103 is what actually carries the deep-submicron parasitics of SG13G2. Production numbers need a simulator that can load the compiled Verilog-A/ADMS plugin: Xyce, Sandia's open-source parallel SPICE. Free, at least, unlike the tools I got locked out of in October.

**Mar 5** — Except Xyce and the compiled plugin are Linux-only and my dev machine is Windows. Rather than dual-boot or maintain a parallel Windows build, put Xyce under WSL and wrote a small path-translation layer (`D:\...` → `/mnt/d/...`) so the rest of the code never has to know it's talking across an OS boundary. Launched through `bash -c` from Python. Kept the backend file-based on purpose — write a .cir, run Xyce, parse the .prn — instead of an in-process binding: for a tool whose whole premise is that nothing is a black box, every simulation should leave a re-runnable artifact on disk. Worth the I/O overhead.

## April 2026

**Apr 3** — Side quest: built Harmonic Balance as a second, frequency-domain route to the same steady state, working from YalRF's collocation approach as the reference rather than deriving it cold — Fourier ansatz, spectral differentiation (multiply by ±kω0 instead of differencing in time), nonlinear device currents through an IFFT/FFT collocation, Newton on the coefficient vector. Clean on the near-sinusoidal linear tank. Pushed at the real hard-switching cross-coupled pair and watched it need an impractical harmonic count before it stopped ringing at every switching edge — Gibbs phenomenon, exactly as advertised. Kept HB in the toolkit (right engine for distortion/mixer work later, shares the same monodromy machinery) but decided the production 10.25 GHz numbers come from the time-domain path. Knowing when to stop polishing a side quest turns out to also be part of the job.

**Apr 11** — Realized, later than I should have, that this ODE is properly stiff — the ~10.25 GHz carrier and the Q/π-cycle amplitude settling live many orders of magnitude apart. Moved settling onto Backward Euler (unconditionally stable, happily over-damped, wrong for precision waveform capture, exactly right for beating a transient into steady state fast). Moved waveform capture onto TR-BDF2 — second order, L-stable, with a cheap embedded error estimate so the step size grows through smooth stretches and shrinks through the switching edges on its own. Swapped dense linear algebra for sparse LU once the per-step Newton solves started dominating runtime.

## May 2026

**May 6** — First real end-to-end run on the actual Xyce/PSP103 netlist — PSS through the multi-part noise breakdown, stages one through five. PSS converges cleanly to 10.25 GHz. The multi-part noise analyzer puts the dominant 1/f² contribution at 1 MHz offset on the tail current source and the switching pair — matches intuition, matches Hajimiri's own picture, roughly matches what Leeson's old closed-form equation predicts too, for a sanity check, even though Leeson's noise factor and flicker corner are fit parameters and mine aren't. Good.

What it also told me, unprompted: the varactor/tuning path was carrying roughly a third of the near-carrier noise budget, because anything sitting on Vtune gets multiplied straight through by Kvco into frequency noise — exactly the tuning-range/phase-noise tension from the very first week of reading, now an actual percentage in an actual chart.

**May 9** — The idea. The same ISF that says a noise impulse at the zero-crossing produces the worst possible phase kick, and one at the voltage peak produces almost none, doesn't care whether the impulse is unwanted noise or a deliberate charge injection from switching a digital tuning code. Gate every retuning update — the switched-cap word — to fire only inside a narrow window around the voltage peaks, where Γ(θ)≈0, and the switching transient itself should land almost entirely in the amplitude direction, which the circuit corrects on its own, instead of the phase direction, which it never does. Sketched a phase-safe update window, and further out, a fully varactorless version leaning on switched-cap-plus-phase-safe-timing alone for the fine steering a continuous varactor used to do. MEGHADUT (the drone telemetry internship) started around here too, so this became strictly an evenings-and-weekends thread for a while.

**May 13** — The phase-safe-window idea from four days ago deserves more than a paragraph, which means an actual digital control loop, not just a sketch. Started generating the RTL for it instead of hand-writing Verilog from scratch — a small generator that emits the AFC and AAC finite-state machines, plus their SDC timing constraints, straight from the same parameters the analog side already uses, so the two halves of the design can't quietly drift apart from each other over time. Left myself the same note I'd apparently needed once before, from back when a milestone file called something like `M1_simulation_backend` was the entire roadmap this project had: new exception types, new modules, new anything, only when a real piece of code actually needs them — never speculatively, never on the strength of a plan. Much easier to write a wishlist of imports than to keep every name in it backed by an actual file. Didn't fully trust myself on the second half of that yet.

**May 22** — The full varactorless redesign: pull the continuous analog varactor out of the tank entirely and retune magnetically instead, through a transformer-coupled secondary whose coupling — and therefore the tank's effective inductance — gets steered by a current rather than a voltage sitting across a lossy junction capacitance. Re-sized the tank around it: L down to about 120 pH, C up over 2 pF to hold the same 10.25 GHz center, Q around 18. Then pointed the exact same nine-stage extractor at the new netlist, the one I'd been running against the varactor version since December. That reuse is the entire payoff of having built a pipeline eight months ago instead of a one-off script. Baseline varactor-path ISF RMS had been sitting around 0.14; the magnetically-tuned path came back around 0.014 — a full order of magnitude down, on the same code, no separate derivation, no benefit of the doubt. The tuning-range/phase-noise tension I first read as a paragraph on someone's lecture slide in September is, eight months later, a ten-times number I produced myself and can defend.

**May 29** — Two numbers I wanted settled before trusting the redesign with anything else, so I wrote a short, deliberately ugly standalone script — no pipeline, no JSON handoff between stages, just one Python file with two functions in it — to hand-check both before letting the full Xyce-driven extractor anywhere near them. First: the magnetically-tuned phase-noise floor at 1 MHz, folding the new Γrms and the tank's own thermal floor through the Hajimiri integral by hand. Second, unrelated except that I wanted it answered in the same sitting: once this clock leaves the VCO it has to cross into a divided, far slower digital domain to be sampled by anything downstream, and that crossing needs an actual synchronizer, not an assumption. Ran the standard two-flop Gray-code FIFO synchronizer MTBF calculation — a 130 nm regeneration time constant, a 20 ps metastability window, a 1.28 GHz receiving-domain clock off an eighth of the VCO rate — and got a mean time between failures comfortably past 10¹² years, which is exactly the shape an answer to that question should have: either absurdly large or a real problem, nothing in between. The quick script's own closed-form jitter estimate didn't quite land on the full pipeline's actual number, so I hard-coded the pipeline's figure into a comment and left it, because that number was already validated three other ways and didn't need a fourth, uglier one competing with it. Started the CML output buffer the synchronizer sits behind that same afternoon — current-mode logic, because anything switching this fast into an external load wants controlled edge rates and a fixed common-mode, not a rail-to-rail CMOS chain fighting its own ground bounce.

## June 2026

**Jun 4** — Built the Verilog-A behavioral model generator. First pass advanced phase with a fixed ω·Δt each step — fine standalone, fell apart the moment I dropped it into a closed-loop PLL testbench, because once Vtune starts moving, the "constant" ω isn't constant, and a fixed-step phase advance has no way to track that. Fixed it properly: `phase_inst = 2π·idt(freq_inst, 0)` — integrate the genuinely time-varying instantaneous frequency instead of faking it with a static step, the only way phase stays continuous once the loop closes. Checked the fix against an existing open-source Verilog-A VCO model afterward and found it uses exactly the same idt-based accumulator — a relief, since it meant I'd converged on the standard correct pattern rather than a personal reinvention of it, and a useful reminder that whatever credit was actually owed here belonged to what came next, not to the fix itself. Weighted the injected stochastic phase noise by the extracted PPV at the wrapped instantaneous phase, so the fast behavioral model carries the same cyclostationary sensitivity as the slow transistor-level netlist instead of a flat white-noise floor.

**Jun 10** — Jitter integration, stage six. First pass integrated the phase-noise spectrum straight down toward fm=0 and watched the RMS jitter number head to infinity, which any 1/f² or 1/f³ power law will always do with no lower bound. Obvious in hindsight, mildly embarrassing in the moment. Fix isn't numerical, it's a decision: an RMS jitter number means nothing without stating the band. Tied fmin to the intended PLL loop bandwidth and fmax to the Nyquist frequency, and made the pipeline refuse to report a jitter figure without both attached.

**Jun 19** — PVT corner sweep — mutate the netlist across TT/FF/SS, 1.08/1.20/1.32 V, and −40 °C to 125 °C. Final table:

| Corner | VDD | Temp | f0 | PN @ 1 MHz |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TT | 1.20 V | 27 °C | 10.25 GHz | −130 dBc/Hz |
| FF | 1.32 V | 125 °C | 10.41 GHz | −131 dBc/Hz |
| SS | 1.08 V | −40 °C | 10.09 GHz | −128 dBc/Hz |

−130 dBc/Hz at 1 MHz on a ~10 GHz carrier is the number I'd read about back in September as a fairly ordinary bar. Landing right on it at the nominal corner, nine months and one homemade solver later, felt disproportionate for what is, on paper, just hitting a spec. The phase-safe-window variant validated too — the tuning-path contribution that sat around 35% of the near-carrier budget in the baseline collapsed to essentially nothing once retuning was confined to the safe window.

**Jun 21** — Going back through the case-study figures for the write-up, noticed some of them are quietly plotting the phase-safe-window variant while the surrounding text still says "the V1 VCO." Not really a typo, more a symptom of how the project actually moved — I kept regenerating plots from whichever netlist was freshest in the working directory, and by June the freshest one wasn't the original. Leaving a couple of those as-is rather than scrubbing every trace of it. It's an honest record of a design that changed mid-project because the tool told me something I hadn't built it to tell me.

**Jun 24** — Verifying the generated RTL the way I'd insist on for anyone else's — by simulating it, not by reading it and hoping. Brought in cocotb, Python-driven coroutine testbenches instead of another dialect of self-checking Verilog, and wrote one verification harness per block: AFC alone, AAC alone, the CML buffer alone, the VCO wrapper alone, the PLL loop alone. Each with its own clock, its own reset sequence, its own pass/fail report. Slower than one big smoke test up front, but it means knowing exactly which block is lying the moment something fails — and something always fails first, usually the reset sequencing, never the part I was actually worried about.

**Jun 27** — Full-system integration, `tb_top`, every block wired together the way it will actually sit on the die.
